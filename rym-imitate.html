Kind of Blue (1959)

Miles Davis

Reviewed by mookid

My theory for why this album is so widely loved is because it is maybe the best example of a specific mood that a lot of people want from a jazz album, the cool smoky nocturnal urban thing. Even despite these songs all becoming jazz standards, it's not instantly a stunner. But the fact that it was in the genuinely forward-thinking new style of modal jazz yet also managed to be easily appreciated by people who have no idea what that means is a testament to its brilliance. The players are all so locked in with each other that even though they're real hushed they sound like the only band playing in the whole world. It's simply an easy album to put on, with magic to the compositions and performances while also being really approachable, which is why it's never really left my general rotation and since high school whenever the exact right 3am moment has come along this has been there for me, and after having that experience enough times I started to realize why this is considered an all-timer. You are doing yourself a huge disservice and are plainly misinformed if you think it's only for jazz beginners; it is beloved by anyone who appreciates the art form.

Seventh Son of a Seventh Son(1988)

Iron Maiden

Reviewed by Janzon

My favorite Maiden album, Seventh Son of a Seventh Son skillfully mixes the epic with the catchy, and I'm pleasantly surprised with the amounts of hooks, refrains and riffs that I actually enjoy. The problem, though, is the same as always - not just with this band but a lot of heavy metal - and that is the vocals. Why must they sound like hilariously bad actors starring in an over-the-top-dramatic fantasy flick? Anyways, good music. And I never thought I would say that about an Iron Maiden album, since I almost never say that about any metal album. Favorite song: The Evil That Men Do
